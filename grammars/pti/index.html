Consonants

Coarticulation series

Proto-Tiengic stop and nasal consonants occur at three primary places of articulation, in plain forms and with two coarticulations. These coarticulation series are summarised in the table below.

Proto-Tiengic has a vertical vowel inventory, with only three phonemic vowel qualities. There is however extensive allophony, with each vowel phoneme having two allophones that it assimilates to when following a coarticulated consonant. The phonemic vowels and their allophones are presented in the table to the right.

The palatal allophones occur when the vowel follows a palatalised consonant or /j/. Similarly, the rounded allophones occur when the vowel follows a rounded consonant or /w/.

The vowel phonemes are romanised as i e a /ɨ ɘ ɐ/ and allophonic alternations are not indicated.

Syllabic consonants

Nasal consonants /m n ŋ/ can be syllabified and act as syllable nuclei. The same is not true of their coarticulated forms (/mʲ nʲ nʷ/ etc.), which, if syllabified, will simply result in epenthesis of a vowel following the nasal, /ɨ/ for palatal nasals and /ɘ/ for rounded nasals.

consonantal m mʲ n nʲ nʷ ŋ ŋʲ ŋʷ
syllabic m̩ mɨ n̩ nɨ nɘ ŋ̩ ŋɨ ŋɘ

The syllabic nasals are romanised identically to non-syllabic nasals and their syllabification must be inferred from syllable structure.

Syllable structure

({C, SF}) (L1) V (L2) (C)

Elements enclosed in braces and comma-separated represent an "either-or" selection, e.g. {C, SF} means either any consonant (C) or a stop followed by a fricative (SF).

  • S is a stop, i.e. one of /p t k b d g/ including all variant coarticulations
  • F is a fricative, i.e. either /s/ or /z/
  • L is a liquid, i.e. one of /l r j w/

Voicing contrasts are prohibited in SF(L) onsets, with the stop assimilating in voicing with the fricative.

L1 and L2 are mutually exclusive, i.e. a liquid cannot occur in both the onset and coda.

Stress

Stress is not contrastive and can be regularly determined from syllable weights. Stress will fall on the heavier of either the ultimate or penultimate syllable if the antepenultimate syllable is light, otherwise falling on the antepenultimate.

Syllable weights are determined as follows.

  1. All syllables start with a weight of 0.
  2. Syllables with a coda are given +1 to their weight.
  3. Syllables with a complex onset are given +1 to their weight.
  4. Syllables with a glide in the onset are given -1 to their weight.
  5. Syllables with a glide in the coda are given +1 to their weight.

Syllable reduction

Syllable reduction is a common morphological process where syllables are altered to reduce their weight. The exact steps of reduction may differ in certain circumstances, so the most common reduction method is given here. Where a different reduction method is used, it will be described and syllable reduction can otherwise be assumed to follow these steps.

CV
Coarticulations on the consonant are removed. Can be fully reduced to ʔV but also sometimes left unchanged.
CVC
If the final consonant is a stop, it is dropped (reduced to CV).
If the final consonant is a nasal, it is reduced to a glide (N → l,r, Nʲ → j, Nʷ → w) or dropped.
If the final consonant is a glide it undergoes metathesis with the vowel and the consonant will frequently reduce to ʔ or have its coarticulations removed (reduction to CLV).
CLVC
The final consonant is dropped or reduced to ʔ.
If the first consonant is not a stop, the glide is dropped.
CVLC
The final consonant is dropped or reduced to ʔ.
The first consonant loses any coarticulations and if it is a fricative typically fortitions into a stop.
If the glide is one of y w it undergoes metathesis with the vowel, or is otherwise lost.

Sandhi

Adjacent consonants will dissimilate in coarticulation, with the preceding consonant losing its coarticulation.

Morphology

Verbs and verb phrases

Verb classes

Proto-Tiengic verbs are broadly divided into six classes, with class assignment being primarily semantic. These classes conflate with transitivity, with each class having a default transitivity and productive morphosyntactic valency-changing operations.

The classes are given below.

motion
These verbs describe motion from one position or point to another (e.g. stand up, arrive, leave) are by default intransitive.
emotion
These verbs describe mental states (e.g. think, be angry, concentrate) and are by default intransitive.
transfer
These verbs are those which involve transfer of ownership (e.g. give, take, buy, sell, provide) or reciprocal motion (e.g. swap, spin). These verbs are by default transitive.
creation
These verbs describe actions of creation (e.g. build, pile up, connect, shape) and are by default transitive. This class also contains certain verbs wherein the object changes state such as clean or causative open.
state
These verbs describe states of being, including predicative adjectives, and are by default intransitive.
process
These verbs describe processes and are by default intransitive.

Proto-Tiengic has a number of tense and aspect categories, with the two categories being conflated in some inflections. While tense and aspect are primarily indicated with fusional affixes, mood categories are always distinct, being indicated primarily with auxiliaries following the verb.

While all verb classes share a past and non-past distinction, not all classes distinguish the same set of aspects. The table to the side shows the sets of aspects each class distinguishes and the listing below provides definitions for each of the aspectual abbreviations.


pfv
Perfective. This aspect is used for actions that are complete, which can be completed, or which have an endpoint.
ipfv
Imperfective. This aspect is used for actions that are incomplete or ongoing, or which cannot be completed or do not have an endpoint.
inch
Inchoative. This aspect is used for actions that are beginning.
punc
Punctual. This aspect is used for actions that are instantaneous or take place very quickly. Occasionally the punctual and inchoative aspects take on a similar distinction to the perfective and imperfective aspects, with inchoative indicating the beginning of an incomplete action and the punctual indicating the beginning of a complete action.
iter
Iterative. This aspect is used for actions that happen repetitively, either many times in quick succession or at regular intervals. It is not typically used for actions that occur irregularly over longer timespans.

Valency-changing operations on motion verbs

Verbs in the motion class have a large set of valency-increasing auxiliaries that introduce a target of motion. The semantic nature of the introduced argument depends on the combination of the verb and the auxiliary used.

Certain auxiliaries require that the introduced argument is in the construct state.

As these auxiliaries precede the verb, they will immediately follow the introduced argument, lending themselves to reinterpretation as locative postclitics in daughters.

dʲa indicates the direction or final destination of motion. The introduced argument is in the construct state.

mʲe-ʔe zu-kʲu-ʔ dʲa nʷekʲ-lyey 1-PL river<3.CNS> to stand_up-INCH.NPST We're leaving to go to the river now.

Other valency-changing operations

One of Proto-Tiengic's most widely used valency-changing devices is the applicative suffix -pʷer which introduces an argument. The role of the argument depends on the verb in question, but is usually the semantic patient.

example example example

On motion verbs, the suffix usually takes a causative meaning. When used on certain verbs it can cause the verb class to change, as seen in pre-causative motion and post-causative creation wherein the verb 'to open' shifts to the creation class when the applicative is used.

pre-causative motion yedi mak-kʲan 3S open-PFV.NPST It opens He opens (it). post-causative creation yedi mʲiyad mak-kʲay-pʷer 3S door open-PFV.NPST-APPL He opens the door.

Nouns and noun phrases

Number

Nouns mark for two numbers, plural and collective plural, with unmarked nouns being in the singular.

The plural is marked by reduplicating the final syllable and reducing it (see syllable reduction). The collective plural is marked with a postposition, ge. The plural and collective plural are not mutually exclusive, as can be seen in pl_and_col.

kʷaydi-ʔi warrior~PL warriors kʷaydi ge warrior COL army pl_and_col kʷaydi-ʔi ge warrior~PL COL armies pʲan-pal finger~PL fingers hands-1 pʲan ge finger COL hand hands-2 pʲan-pal ge finger~PL COL hands

Note that 'hands' in hands-1 and hands-2 means hands in a flat, palms up position.

Possession

Rather than marking for a possessive, Proto-Tiengic take marking if they are possessed, the so-called construct state. This marking is also used in certain syntactic situations where it indicates that the noun has certain syntactic dependents. The marking agrees with the person of the dependent introduced.

It is marked with an infix, reduplicating the nucleus of the final syllable before the coda with an infixed consonant.

kʷaydi bʷirk pʲan ʔekʷ
1 cns -y kʷaydiyi bʷiyirk pʲayan ʔeyekʷ
2 cns -t kʷayditi bʷitirk pʲatan ʔetekʷ
3 cns -kʲ kʷaydikʲi bʷigʲirk pʲakʲan ʔegʲekʷ

As can be seen above, the kʲ in the third person will lose its coarticulation if it precedes another coarticulated consonant and will occasionally dissimilate in voicing with the coda consonant it is inserted before.

These infixes do not affect the syllable reduplicated in plural marking.

Personal pronouns

Proto-Tiengic distinguishes only the first and second persons in personal pronouns, with pronouns inflecting as nouns do. These pronouns are presented in the table to the right.

In lieu of third person personal pronouns, Proto-Tiengic has a large, closed set of basic nouns that serve as prototypes of various categories and are used as anaphora for other noun phrases in their category. For the purposes of analysis, these classifier pronouns are considered to be separate from but homophonous with their respective nouns (and in some cases the classifier pronouns are reduced versions of the normal noun).

For example, in classifier pronoun example, yedi is used as anaphor for a human third person referent and nagʷ for a long, thin, flat third person referent, here a knife blade. In casual speech ŋeʔ 'knife' would be elided as it is the purpose of the anaphor and context to disambiguate.

Interrogative and indefinite pronouns

Proto-Tiengic has a single series of interrogative pronouns and three series of indefinite pronouns, fulfulling different functions. The indefinite pronoun series are derived from the interrogative pronoun series. These series distinguish 8 ontological categories: person, thing, place, direction, kind, time, amount, and reason.

person thing place direction kind time amount reason

yenʲ-series yenʲ-na yenʲ-lak yen-dʷye yenʲ-ayla yenʲ-iza

Interrogatives can function as full nouns, or as adjectives on classifier pronouns.

lak tik ʔsar-yek what 2S be_holding-IPFV What are you holding? bʷziʔ lak tik lwir-kʲan bowl what 2S bring-PFV.PST Which dish did you bring?

The bʷay-series is also used for non-specific referents in irrealis clauses, overlapping in usage with the ki-series - in these situations, usage of the bʷay-series tends to be more poetic or formal. It is also used in the protasis of conditional clauses, as in conditional protasis, and as the standard of comparison in comparative clauses, as in comparative standard.

The bʷay-series lends itself to further extension into the functions of the ki-series, taking on specific known and specific unknown functions, distinguishing itself from the ki-series in referential specificity.

The free-choice function of indefinite pronouns is achieved using universal quantifiers.

Syntax

Proto-Tiengic is a strongly head-final language, with an SOV primary word order and adjectives preceding nouns.

Adjectives and adverbs

Adjectives precede the nouns they modify and multiple adjectives may be used to modify a single noun. Adjectives are universally single words (including associated morphology) and multiple-word phrases as nominal modifiers are not permitted (excepting relative clauses).

mʲla yedi tall person A tall person.

Similarly, verbs permit simple adverbs that precede them.

yedi mʲla nsa-kʲan 3S tall fall-PFV.PST He fell from a height.

Proto-Tiengic also permits more complex adverbial phrases, with a large set of adverbialisers that follow the introduced adverbial constituent and indicate the way it modifies the verb.

A list of adverbialisers is given in the appendices.

yedi nʷire dʲa yedi bʷi-gʲi-rk zyi ʔigʷeʔ-kʲan 3S home to 3S mother<CNS> BEN leave-PFV.PST He went home because of his mother.

Simple clauses

A syntax tree describing the structure of simple clauses in Proto-Tiengic is given to the right. Notable in this tree is the element Pol, the polarity marker. This is a marker that occupies the specifier position of the verb phrase and marks the polarity of the clause. In affirmative clauses it has no phonological realisation. This element will be described in greater detail below.

The most simple clauses in Proto-Tiengic consist of a verbal predicate and one or two noun phrases as arguments. simple intransitive illustrates the use of a single argument with an intransitive verb to form a simple intransitive clause. Similarly, simple transitive illustrates a simple transitive clause.

simple intransitive yedi ge nʷekʲ-n person COL AFF stand_up-IPFV.NPST The crowd is standing up. simple transitive mʲe bʷir-yi-k lyeʔ-le ge dya-ŋʷe 1S mother<1.CNS> AFF rice~PL COL cook-REP.NPST Mother is cooking up all the rice.

Polarity marking

Proto-Tiengic clauses mark for polarity using the Pol constituent in the clause structure. The slot is occupied by a single particle that indicates the clause's polarity. As the polarity marker occupies the specifier position of the verb phrase it can only occur once per clause and has no dependents.

The most common form of the polarity marker is the affirmative (aff) which has no overt phonetic realisation.

In negative polarity clauses the polarity marker has the form kʷi.

mʲe bʷir-yi-k kʷi lyeʔ-le ge dya-ŋʷe 1S mother<1.CNS> NEG rice~PL COL cook-REP.NPST Mother is not cooking up all the rice.

Syntactic valency alternations

Alongside morphological valency-changing operations, Proto-Tiengic's verb classes have a number of syntactic methods for changing their valency.

Auxiliary verb constructions

Proto-Tiengic has a large collection of auxiliary verbs that typically indicate modality or aspect (alongside aspectual marking on the lexical verb). Auxiliaries form an auxiliary phrase with the lexical verb, which they become the head of, causing the lexical verb to shift to before the object as it becomes an adjunct of the auxiliary phrase. TAM markers on the lexical verb are additionally transferred to the auxiliary.

mʲe iʔ dya-nʲiy 1S AFF food cook-IPFV.NPST I am cooking food. avc example mʲe dya iʔ law-nʲiy 1S AFF cook food can-IPFV.NPST I can cook food.

Proto-Tiengic allows omission of the lexical verb if it is recoverable from context, such as in coordinated clauses or in a response to a question.

mʲe kʷey law-nʲiy aʔ yedi law-nʲiy 1S swim can-IPFV.NPST and 3S can-IPFV.NPST I can swim and so can she.

A list of auxiliaries is given in the appendices.

Polar questions

Proto-Tiengic forms polar questions using a particle, za, at the end of the corresponding declarative clause.

yedi subʷ-su dzur-kʲay-pʷer za 3S clothing~PL wash-PFV.PST-APPL Q Did he wash the clothes?

Content questions

Proto-Tiengic forms content questions using its interrogative pronoun series. Interrogative pronouns can function as nouns or as adjectives modifying classifier pronouns.

Introduction of an interrogative pronoun as an argument, although a valency-increasing operation, requires no overt valency-changing markers.

Proto-Tiengic exhibits wh-fronting and pied-piping with interrogative pronouns, with the phrase that the interrogative is a part of being moved to the front of the clause.

Wh-fronting results in transferral of the VP to a complement phrase (CP) and raising of the Pol constituent to the head of the complement phrase. This raising can also be seen in subordination and construction of relative clauses.

tik Ø ʔsar-yek 2S AFF be_holding-IPFV You are holding (it). wh-fronting lak Ø tik ʔsar-yek what AFF 2S be_holding-IPFV What are you holding?

Long-distance content questions

Proto-Tiengic allows double extraction of an interrogative pronoun or phrase from within an embedded clause in long-distance questions. This construction, however, also requires the polar question particle za in the matrix clause.

lak yedi-ʔi Ø Ø mʲeʔe ŋʲiy-yek kʷwan-pi-pʷer za what person~PL AFF AFF 1P want-IPFV think-IPFV-APPL Q What do they think we want?

Subordination and relative clauses

Independent clauses are transformed into dependent clauses via raising of the Pol constituent to the head of the complement phrase, allowing embedding of clauses and construction of relative clauses.

Once a clause has been transformed into a dependent clause it can simply be used as an argument in an independent clause.

As in coordination, there is a volitional pivot, allowing the noun phrase with the highest agency to be omitted in the embedded clause if it is shared with the matrix clause.

mʲe Ø Ø (mʲe) kʷey-lyey ŋʲiy-yek 1S AFF AFF (1S) swim-INCH.NPST want-IPFV I want to go swimming.

In practice, only one of the polarity markers is likely to be phonetically overt at any given time and, since they are adjacent, whichever one it is is indeterminate. This could result in merging of the two polarity markers, effectively transforming the clause into a normal independent clause with no subordinate.

The subordination transformation renders a correct independent clause structure for both transitive and intransitive subordinates. This lends itself to reinterpretation as a serial verb construction in daughters.

Relative clauses use the same subordination structure with the head of the relative clause being extracted to the matrix clause and replaced in the relative clause with the appropriate pronoun. As with adjectives, relative clauses precede the nouns they modify.

Proto-Tiengic permits only core arguments of the relative clause to act as its head, restricting the role of the head in the relative clause to either the subject, object, or indirect object. Because of this restriction, the applicative finds use in relative clauses in raising oblique arguments to core roles and allowing relativisation.

mʲe Ø yedi mʲe la-ya-tʲ n-yek yedi gʲir-zaw 1S AFF 3S 1S father<CNS> be-IPFV person see-IPFV.NPST I see the person who is my father.

Coordination

Proto-Tiengic has a large set of coordinators that specify the relationship between the coordinated constituents. A list of these coordinators is givev in the appendices.

Perhaps the simplest of these coordinators is zde, corresponding to English 'and'.

mʲe bʷir-yi-k zde mʲe la-ya-tʲ 1S mother<CNS> and 1S father<CNS> My mother and my father.

Where the two constituents of the coordination are clauses, Proto-Tiengic allows omission of the argument of the highest volition in the second clause if it is shared between the clauses.

mʲe yedi madʷi-tey zde nsa-kʲan 1S 3S hit-PFV.PST and fall-PFV.PST I hit it and I fell. I hit it and it fell.
