Proto-Lahiri language

Proto-Lahiri is the linguistic reconstruction of the most recent common ancestor of the Lahiri languages, a family of languages distributed across Lahan and parts of Puzimm.

Most estimates of Proto-Lahiri's age have it spoken as a single language around 4000 BCE, although, due to the uncertainty of the comparative method, this date varies between estimates by more than a thousand years. Due to the presence of various archaeological sites around Puzimm and Proto-Lahiri's commonly-accepted relation to the neighbouring Adzo-Neviric languages in north and eastern Baredina, the original homeland of Proto-Lahiri's speakers is believed to have been somewhere in Puzimm.

Genetic evidence suggests Proto-Lahiri speakers migrated across the Lahan Sea somewhere between 4000 and 3500 BCE, and successive migrations split the language and people into various groups. Following various gradual shifts in phonology and morphology, the languages of these groups diverged and the Proto-Lahiri language transformed into ancient Lahiri languages, and, following further development, the modern Lahiri language family.


The Proto-Lahiri speakers were a semi-nomadic people, with the reconstructed lexicon suggesting a lifestyle heavily dependent upon the sea. Limited agricultural terms and archaeological sites suggest the Proto-Lahiri speakers were seasonally dependent upon agriculture, a period during which they would become sedentary.

Proto-Lahiri's large vocabulary around storytelling and genealogy suggests a strong oral tradition; this is reflected in some branches of the family which also feature strong oral histories and a collective body of myth shared between many languages and ethnic groups.

Syllable structure

Proto-Lahiri has, at first glance, a somewhat complex syllable structure, allowing maximally CCCVVCCC syllables. This is made considerably more manageable by the various constraints placed upon the sequences of consonants in the onset and coda which are permissible.

Stress

Stress in Proto-Lahiri is contrastive and indicated with an acute accent. Stress placement on monosyllables is trivial and so is not indicated. As derivational and inflectional morphemes rarely take stress, polysyllabic words where the stem is monosyllabic may be found to have no stress marking - these can be assumed to be stressed on the stem.

náátip /ˈnä̰.tɨp/ force, power naatíp /nä̰ˈtɨp/ deceive, lie to síveb /ˈsɨ.βəb/ lie down sivéb /sɨˈβəb/ sleep

Morphology

Nouns and noun phrases

Proto-Lahiri classifies all nouns by three categories: register, animacy, and number class. All nouns have an inherent value for each of these categories which affects nominal morphology, verbal agreement, and clause structure, among other things. Various methods exist to change a noun's value for each of these categories.

Register corresponds roughly with formality or politeness. Three registers exist: neutral, polite, and vulgar. The register used for a given noun reflects the speaker's attitude towards the noun.

Proto-Lahiri has a complex animacy system, centered around a large animacy hierarchy, upon which all nouns are located. Nouns are located fairly predictably on the hierarchy based on their semantics, however register-changing alternations may cause nouns to move up or down the hierarchy.

The animacy hierarchy features prominently in Proto-Lahiri's syntax and affects how verbs assign thematic roles to their arguments. The hierarchy is given, in order, below. Levels on the hierarchy are referred to by their number with g- prefixed, e.g. g1.

  1. Second person personal pronouns
  2. First person personal pronouns
  3. Third person personal pronouns
  4. Religious (gods; priests; other sacred or divine nouns)
  5. Adult sentient beings
  6. Flying animals (birds; bats; excludes bugs which occur lower)
  7. Large animals (including flightless birds)
  8. Medium animals (including young sentient beings such as children)
  9. Small animals
  10. Moderate (fire; liquids; body parts)
  11. Large plants
  12. Small plants
  13. Constructed inanimates (structures; tools)
  14. Bugs (most small invertebrates)
  15. Terrain (rocks; landscape features)
  16. Uncouth (parasites; disease; natural disasters)

Personal pronouns are not typically placed on the hierarchy but have a position and order in it and behave similarly to other nouns with respect to it, hence are included here for clarity.

Number class determines what form of number marking a noun may take. Three number classes exist: count, non-count, and mass. Count nouns take full number marking, non-count nouns take no marking whatsoever, and mass nouns indicate number through classifiers. Mass nouns form a semantic class, whereas assignment to count or non-count is arbitrary.

Number

Proto-Lahiri nouns mark for three numbers: singular, paucal, and plural. The paucal is used for "a few" of a noun, with what counts as "a few" being largely determined by context and the noun in question.

The number markers are a set of prefixes, exhibiting significant variation based on the animacy of the noun being marked.

Possession

Possession is head-marked, being indicated on the possessed noun with one of a set of affixes that agrees with the person, number, and animacy of the possessor. The possessor follows the possessed noun.

Possessive inflection occurs after number inflection, so possessive morphemes occur externally to number morphemes.

Verbs and verb phrases

Evidentiality

Proto-Lahiri verbs mark for three evidential categories. These categories are marked with simple prefixes. As with nominal marking, aa is inserted between the prefix and the verb stem where it would create an illegal syllable.

The direct evidential is used for events experienced directly, such as by sight or touch. This includes "direct" sensory experience such as hearing something that can also be seen, but does not include "indirect" sensory experience such as hearing something that is not within the speaker's line of sight. It is marked with the prefix s-.

direct minimal víe s-aa-saawét wit ki 1S DIR-E-look.at PFV G6 I have seen it.

The indirect evidential is used for events experienced indirectly. This includes indirect sensory experience such as hearing things out of line of sight, and other indirect knowledge such as through hearsay. It is marked with the prefix n-.

direct minimal and indirect minimal form a minimal pair, illustrating the distinction between the direct and indirect evidentials.

indirect minimal víe n-aa-saawét wit ki 1S IDR-E-look.at PFV G6 I have seen it (indirectly, e.g. in a picture). éntaa n-aa-taawéni t-aa-kélj j-aa-déhek G2 IDR-E-give.birth 1S.CNS-E-hear PL-E-two I heard she had twins.

The inferential evidential is used for any a priori knowledge, such as inferences, logical deductions, or assumptions. It is indicated with the prefix ji-. It has the form j- on vowel initial words.

ji-kééls lekáá INFR-rain EXP I can tell it has been raining.

Moods

Proto-Lahiri distinguishes three moods in verbal marking: realis, which is the default unmarked form of a verb; irrealis, used for a wide variety of situations that are not or have yet to be realised; and dubitative, a subtype of irrealis which indicates uncertainty or doubt as to whether an event will occur.

The realis is the basic form of any verb stem, being unmarked and relatively semantically neutral. It is used for situations that either have already happened, are happening, or are guaranteed to happen.

The irrealis form is used for events are not real, whether they are hypothetical or future events or if the speaker is unsure. Situations where the irrealis mood might be used include in the protasis of conditionals, certain modal expressions, future events which may not happen, and inferential statements for which the speaker has no evidence.

The irrealis is marked with a nasal infix: n is inserted after the last vowel in the word, which is then copied to after the infix after being put through a raising process, which will be described below.

éntaa ji-wéé-nii-yk wit iitexékaa G2 INFR-go.to<IRR> PFV home He might have gone home.

In raising, vowels shift upwards, becoming the next higher vowel in the case of /ä ə/, which become /ə ɨ/ respectively, or cracking into /jə/ in the case of /ɨ/. Vowel phonation is preserved in this process, so /ä ä̰/ become /ə ə̰/ respectively.

The dubitative form, a subtype of the irrealis, is used for events that are not real and which the speaker is uncertain or doubts will become real, and can be used wherever the irrealis is. It is marked by reduplicating the first vowel in the word to the start of the word, between the stem and the evidential prefix, raising the first vowel in the stem (i.e. the original vowel that was reduplicated), and suffixing -s, inserting aa if this would violate the word's phonotactics.

On words that begin in vowels, the initial reduplication is not carried out, however the first vowel is still raised in place.

éntaa j-ee-wííyk-s wit iitexékaa G2 INFR-DUB-go.to-DUB PFV home He might have gone home. (but I doubt it)

Modals

Proto-Lahiri's modals makes their primary distinction in modal base, leaving modal force lexically unspecified. There are three modal particles which situate before the verb.

The modal particle ki has its modal base in capability - the a priori physical or mental ability of the subject. This is to be distinguished from the epistemic modal le which can also have capabilitive meaning but differs in the origin of capability, with the epistemic specifying ability from knowledge and the capabilitive ki specifying inherent ability. For this reason, ki is rarely used with human or sentient subjects as human ability is considered to derive from knowledge rather than inherent ability or instinct, as with an animal.

The capabilitive modal ki requires the main verb to be marked for the inferential evidential; other evidential markings are considered ungrammatical.

capabilitive mood change twixéw he ki ji-swíícep piiyééa axe PROX CAP INFR-cut.through dense.forest This axe can cut through dense forest. irrealis capabilitive modal twixéw he ki ji-swííce-ni-p piiyééa axe PROX CAP INFR-cut.through<IRR> dense.forest This axe might be able to cut through dense forest. This axe must be able to cut through dense forest.

As Proto-Lahiri's modals do not specify modal force, irrealis capabilitive modal permits alternate translations with varying modal force.

Note the variation in meaning based off of mood marking on the verb in capabilitive mood change. In the realis, as the situation is real, only one interpretation is valid, in which the axe is definitely able to cut through dense forest. However, in the irrealis, as the situation becomes hypothetical, two interpretations become valid because of the unspecified modal force.

ki also distinguishes itself from le in permitting circumstantial readings, where le does not. ki allows human or sentient subjects in circumstantial readings, and use of a human or sentient subject with ki tends to cause a circumstantial interpretation.

syiibeeíín ki ji-yiicá x-aa-cééaa ixé reed CAP INFR-grow G12.CNS-E-estuary on.top Reeds can grow in the estuary. (based off of the conditions in the estuary, although there may not actually be any there) syiibeeíín le ji-yiicá x-aa-cééaa ixé reed EPIS INFR-grow G12.CNS-E-estuary on.top Reeds can grow in the estuary. (I know there are actually reeds growing there)

The modal ka expresses deontic modality - what could or must be according to some set of rules or beliefs. As with the other modals, the modal force is unspecified. ka does not enforce any evidential or modal requirements upon the marking of the verb.

dve étih ka ji-hézaa t-aa-káá-vi 1P NEG DEON INFR-be.at 1P.CNS-E-stay-CIRC We cannot stay here. evé ka j-efázi 2S DEON INFR-enter You should come in.

When the verb is marked as irrealis, ka enforces a future interpretation and allows epistemic readings. In these cases it is glossed as fut as it is no longer strictly deontic.

éntaa ka ji-lají-nye G2 FUT come.back<IRR> He might/will come back. evé j-aétka-ne tii peyí ka ji-niiéé-nyee-k 2S INFR-shake<IRR> G10 COND:then FUT INFR-drop.down<IRR> If you disturb the rock it might fall.

Syntax

Primary word order

Proto-Lahiri displays a primary SVO word order, as can be seen in svo order. This is complicated, however, as the order of arguments is determined by their animacy rather than their theta roles, with the core argument higher on the animacy hierarchy occurring before the verb. This results in OVS word order in clauses with an agent of lower animacy than the patient. Such cases are marked with the inverse marker, which indicates the lower animacy argument is the agent; this can be seen in ovs order.

svo order [A jii-tiizdí ] n-aa-péék [P w-aa-kíyp ] G5.CNS-father IDR-E-search.for G2.CNS-E-son He's searching for his son. ovs order [P jii-tiizdí ] n-aa-péék-seey [A w-aa-kíyp ] G5.CNS-father IDR-E-search.for-INV G2.CNS-E-son He's searching for his father.

The ordering of core arguments by their animacy can be illustrated further by the ungrammaticality of ungrammatical word order svo and ungrammatical word order ovs. The argument tiizdí 'father' has noun class g2, higher on the animacy hierarchy than kiyp 'son' which here has animacy g5.

ungrammatical word order svo [A waakíyp ] naapéék [P jiitiizdí ] ungrammatical word order ovs [P waakíyp ] naapéékseey [A jiitiizdí ]

Oblique arguments generally follow the core arguments, although expressions of time and location can be fronted for pragmatic effect, typically as focus or contrastive information.

éntaa dehé ji-syicá-seey itii-íxiihbi [OBL t-aa-tiijá-vi ] G2 PASS INFR-get-INV G10.PL-bread 1.CNS-E-G10-CIRC Bread, she's making it for us. éntaa ji-syicá-seey itii-íxiihbi he [OBL tiivét ki s-aa-jáát ] G2 INFR-get-INV G10.PL-bread PROX day REL DR-E-leave He made this bread yesterday. [OBL t-aa-tiijá-vi ] evé ka ji-syicá-ne itii-íxiihbi yaa 1.CNS-E-G10-CIRC 2S DEON INFR-get<IRR> G10.PL-bread MED You should've made the bread yesterday.

So called 'clausal modifiers' precede the verb, following the subject. This slot contains various modifiers such as negative and modal particles and the passive marker. This slot can contain multiple markers, as in negation alongside a modal, however this is dispreferred and any given category in this slot can be marked only once. These elements are dependent on the verb.

tiijá [VP [POL étih ] [VOICE dehé ] [MOD ka ] s-aa-syicá ] G10 NEG PASS DEON DR-E-get It should not have been taken.

One notable feature of Proto-Lahiri's syntax is the dispreference of iteration on adjunct nodes. It is near impossible, for instance, for multiple locative obliques to occur in the same clause. This is also reflected in the language's lack of root trivalent verbs, with notions such as giving being expressed with a coordinate clause or benefactive oblique.

give construction example éntaa s-pecáá siiyál [OBL w-aa-tiijá w-ecípa ] G2 DR-give knife G2.CNS-E-G10 G2.CNS-sister He gave the knife to his sister.

The analysis of the benefactor of the giving in give construction example as a benefactive oblique and not as a core argument is motivated primarily by its marking as a benefactive, identically to the marking of benefactive obliques in other, prototypically bivalent constructions. In addition, the clause allows omission of the oblique without change in the roles of the core arguments or the clause's overall grammaticality.
