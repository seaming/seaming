Proto-Upper-Borosan

Proto-Upper-Borosan is the linguistic reconstruction of the common ancestor of the Upper-Borosan languages. It should be noted that the Upper-Borosan languages do not include every language spoken in upper Boroso, and there are Upper-Borosan languages spoken in southern Boroso.

Most reconstructions estimate Proto-Upper-Borosan was spoken by a unified group of people around approximately 4000 BCE, although estimates may vary by up to a thousand years. Genetic analyses and limited archaeological evidence suggest that the original homeland of speakers of Proto-Upper-Borosan was somewhere around the southern and eastern shores of Lake Qëërles.

Voiced stops and nasal consonants are in complementary distribution, with the nasals occurring adjacent to nasal vowels and voiced stops occurring elsewhere. This is not distinguished orthographically as where they occur as nasals can be recovered from the environment.

The following orthography will be adopted for ease of typing. Unless noted below, phonemes are written as their IPA.

Ch b d j g ’ z y o u ą ę į ǫ ų
Cʰ m~b n~d ɲ~ɟ ŋ~g ʔ ʃ~ɕ j u ɨ ã ẽ ĩ ũ ɨ̃

Proto-Upper-Borosan permits geminate stops and nasals medially. Phonemically this is analysed as having an intervening syllable break, but is realised as a geminate in the onset of the second syllable.

Syllable structure

(C)(L)V(j)(C)

  • L is one of /l r j w/
  • in complex onsets (i.e. CL) C cannot be in L

Any diphthongs are prohibited; if two vowels come into contact, an intervening glottal stop is inserted.

Stress

Stress placement is irregular and words can contrast only in position of stress. Stress is realised as a slight raise in pitch, relative to the surrounding syllables. It is written with an acute accent.

The vowels /a ã u ũ/ reduce in unstressed syllables, merging to [ə ə̃]. This is not indicated orthographically. Where these vowels occur in positions that will never be stressed, i.e. never realised as anything other than [ə ə̃] and the "original" phoneme cannot be recovered, a is used as a default.

Morphology

Nouns and noun phrases

Nouns inflect for two numbers, singular and plural, and two cases, direct and oblique. The direct case notes nouns with a special relation to the verb, typically that of the agent or patient. This relationship is indicated with "trigger" markers on the verb stem (see ).

It is important to note the distinction between the oblique case and oblique arguments in the sentence. The oblique case here is the unmarked form of nouns and is used for nouns that do not have the relation to the verb indicated by the direct case. In any given clause typically only a single argument is marked in the direct case, with all others being in the unmarked "oblique" case, resulting in core arguments (the subject and object) frequently appearing in the oblique case despite being core arguments. Thus it should be noted that the oblique case does not mark oblique arguments, which are distinguishable through word order and other verbal markings.

The noun's inflectional paradigm is given below.

sg pl
direct -chą -syą
oblique - -seg

These suffixes each have a number of allomorphs, varying significantly with the ending of the word they are applied to.

pha 'river' sg pl
dir phacǫ́ phasą́
obl pha pháseg
dir hon phatę́ca phatís
obl hon phaté phatége
zwę 'river' sg pl
dir zwęchą zwęsi
obl zwę zwęhę
dir hon zwętę́ca zwętís
obl hon zwęté zwętége
  • -chą is realised as
    • -cǫ́ on monosyllables where the vowel is /a ã u ũ/.
    • -cwa where the final vowel is stressed /i ɨ u/.
    • -cąn where the final vowel is stressed /ĩ ɨ̃ ũ/.
    • -’ą where the word ends with /ɲ ɟ c cʰ/. The final consonant is removed.
  • -syą is realised as
    • -są́ on monosyllables where the vowel is /a ã u ũ/.
    • -si where the final vowel is stressed /e ẽ/.
  • -seg is realised as
    • -hę where the word ends in a nasal consonant or vowel.

There is an honorific suffix -té that marks respected or revered things, such as celestial objects, gods, or leaders. This combines with the nominal endings as follows.

  • -té-chą becomes -tę́ca
  • -té-si becomes -tís
  • -té-seg becomes -tége

Pronouns

Proto-Upper-Borosan has three basic pronouns, ę in the first person, wa in the second person, and i in the third person. These basic stems are taken to be in the oblique case and can inflect as any other nouns to form the full pronominal paradigm, given below.

Verbs and verb phrases

Verb classes

Proto-Upper-Borosan verbs are sorted into a number of semantic classes based off of their argument structure - the number and theta roles of the arguments they take. Most of these classes are open and membership of a particular form is determined semantically, however each verb is part of only a single class and there are no productive methods for changing a verb's classification.

The classes of verbs and their argument structure properties are given in the table below.

Root valency Role of intransitive argument
Simple transitive transitive
Locative transitive origin or target of motion
Accusative ambitransitive agent
Ergative ambitransitive patient
Unergative intransitive agent
Unaccusative intransitive patient

Triggers

Verbs take trigger suffixes that mark the role of the argument in the direct case. There are four suffixes, marking core agent and patient arguments, and locative and benefactive obliques. This serves to focus certain arguments, the noun in the direct case being focused, having its role and semantics highlighted in contrast to the backing of arguments whose roles are indicated only by syntactic means.

The agentive trigger indicates that the argument in the direct case is an agent or volitional in the action. This is marked by reduplicating the final consonant, or suffixing -y on vowel-final words, and suffixing -ą.

ról-lą ícwa haláw eat-AT 3S.DIR meat He is eating the meat.

The patientive marker indicates that the argument in the direct case is a patient or involitional in the action. It is marked with the suffix -ąró after reduplication of the final consonant, or suffixing -y on vowel final words. It has allomorphs -Cązi on polysyllabic words ending in CV where C is a fricative or /ʔ/ with deletion of the final syllable, and -Cala on iambs (disyllabic words where the second syllable is stressed).

rol-ląró i haláw-chą eat-PT 3S meat-DIR The meat is being eaten by him.

Applicatives

Proto-Upper-Borosan displays three primary applicative markers that it uses to promote oblique arguments to core argument positions, turning intransitives into transitives and transitives into ditransitives. Each applicative has differing use cases, giving the promoted argument differing semantics, and interacts with the verb classes differently. The applied object is typically inserted before the original object, sometimes with the original object being elided. There is however, no overt marking distinguishing the original and applied objects.

The primary purpose of the applicatives is to promote especially salient or relevant arguments to a more prominent position in the sentence. For this reason the applicative is preferred for particularly animate obliques, with first and second person obliques almost always being promoted by means of an applicative.

The directive applicative, indicated by the suffix -lą’, typically introduces arguments indicating the goal of an action, the location where it happened, or an instrument with which the action was accomplished.

sedág-lą’-’ą ę yáge cook-APPL-AT 1S house I am cooking in the house.

The relational applicative, indicated by the suffix -hur, introduces arguments that are benefactors or malefactors of the action, a stimulus of a psychological or perceptual event, abstract goals of an action, or goal of a speech act.

gę’ú-hur ę dashǫ́-yą ílcwa see-APPL 1S go.uphill-AT 3S.DIR I saw him coming up the hill.

The benefactive applicative, indicated by the suffix -Caj, introduces arguments that are benefactors of an action, typically more directly than the relational applicative such as commands, malefactors of an action, or the goal or source of an adverse event.

thuy-yaj-jąró yáge-chą hórra fall.down-APPL-PT house-DIR storm The house was destroyed in the storm.

Adjectives

In Proto-Upper-Borosan, "true" adjectives form a small, closed class with no productive derivation methods. The most common adjectival forms are null derivations from verbs, forming participle-like meanings. The distinction between true adjectives and other adjectival forms is an important one, with only true adjectives being reasonably considerable as a distinct part of speech.

Adjectives follow the phrases they modify. Most modifying things like adpositions are adjectives in PUB, and most nouns and verbs can either directly act as adjectives or be derived into one.

bará la fish black The black fish. bará męsí fish swim The swimming fish.

In predicative phrases, adjective stems can be used as verbs.

phay-yą́ ęchǫ́ angry-AT 1S.DIR I am angry.

Syntax

Primary word order

Proto-Upper-Borosan displays a primary VSO word order, however as the roles of arguments can be explicitly indicated, variation in this is permitted for pragmatic effect.

Questions

Polar questions

Polar questions are made by moving the verb to the sentence final position and leaving the particle i as a trace.

i bará-syą pha mų męsi-yád Q fish-DIR.PL river in swim-AT Do the fish swim in the river? i bará-seg pha-cǫ́ męsi-khéga Q fish-OBL.PL river-DIR swim-LT In the river, do the fish swim?

Content questions

Content questions are made with mąr 'who, what'. This inflects like any other noun but is fronted to the start of the sentence, bringing any modifiers with it. This structure covers 'who, what, where, why, how' by using the agentive/patientive, agentive/patientive, locative, benefactive, and instrumental triggers respectively.

mąr-cǫ́ mų męsi-khéga bará-seg what-DIR in swim-LT fish-OBL.PL Where do the fish swim? (lit. what do the fish swim in?)

For 'when' questions, mą́si 'when' is simply placed in the sentence initial position.

mą́si męsi-yą́ ę́si pay when swim-AT 1P.DIR FUT When will we go swimming?

Causative constructions

Causative constructions are made with the verb dó’e 'to make, to cause to come about'. The main verb is replaced with dó’e and the verb phrase being transformed is moved to the position after the patient.

V S O ⟶ dó’e S O V
VPT SDIR O ⟶ dó’ePT SDIR V O

də’e-yą́ ę́-si ęhę́ męsí make-AT 1P-DIR 1P swim We made ourselves swim. dó-yązi ę́-si ciré ámę make-PT 1P-DIR leave PERF We were made to leave.

Subordination

Subordination is achieved by adjectivisation of the clause being subordinated. This is done by placing the unmarked form of the main verb of the subordinated phrase at the phrase initial position, before the marked form.

aga-yą́ ę́-si męsí męsi-khéga pha-cǫ́ like-AT 1P-DIR swim swim-LT river-DIR We like swimming in the river.

Relative clauses

Relative clauses are formed as subordinated phrases treated as adjectives, following the nouns they modify. They are correlative, requiring that the phrase being subordinated contains the head noun phrase in the direct case, and they leave a pronoun in the place of the head noun phrase in the main clause.

V SiDIR Oj ⟶ Proi V SiDIR Oj
V Sj OiDIR ⟶ Proi V Sj OiDIR

męsi-yą́ ę́-si il męsi-khéga bará-seg pha-cǫ́ mų swim-AT 1P-DIR 3S swim-LT fish-OBL.PL river-DIR in We are swimming in the river that the fish swim in.

Coordination

Of noun phrases

Coordination is done with the coordinating verb tha. This verb subordinates the second element of the coordination to the first.

Ni Nj ⟶ Ni tha NjDIR

The verb usually takes the agentive trigger, but which trigger is used can indicate the case of individual elements in the coordination when it is used. Whether the first element is direct or oblique is the case of the coordination as a whole.

patientive_coordination uses the patientive trigger since the speaker, the second element in the coordination, is the nonvolitional patient of the action.

dale-yád wacǫ́ tha-yó’ ę hunt-AT 2S.DIR and-AT 1S You and I went hunting. patientive_coordination dale-yád wacǫ́ tha-yąró ę hunt-AT 2S.DIR and-PT 1S You took me hunting.
